万葉仮名 (Manyōgana)

Definition

Manyōgana (万葉仮名) is an early Japanese writing system in which Chinese characters (kanji) were used purely for their phonetic value. Instead of representing meaning, these characters were selected to approximate the sounds of the Japanese language.

This system played a crucial role in the development of the Japanese writing system. Over time, repeated phonetic usage of kanji led to the creation of two simplified syllabaries: hiragana and katakana.

Hiragana evolved from the cursive (草書, sōsho) forms of entire kanji, resulting in smooth and rounded characters. In contrast, katakana originated from selected components of kanji, giving them a more angular and structured appearance.

Katakana Origins

Katakana characters were derived by extracting parts of kanji used in Manyōgana. This process created a more compact and geometric writing system, primarily used today for foreign words, emphasis, and technical terms.

Important Notes

In Manyōgana, a single syllable could be represented by multiple kanji. For example, the sound "ka" could be written using 加, 可, 香, and many others.

Modern kana systems are standardized forms that emerged from these variations, making reading and writing significantly more efficient.

Understanding Manyōgana provides valuable insight into the historical evolution of the Japanese writing system and helps learners better appreciate the structure of kana.
